# product_vision.md

## Vision
This app helps a small trusted circle of real-life friends avoid bad meals and know what to order. It turns personal restaurant memory and trusted taste into recommendations that are actually useful when deciding where to eat.

## Tagline
**Avoid bad meals. Trust your people.**

## Problem statement
People routinely make mediocre dining decisions because the tools they use are fragmented and noisy. Public reviews are generic, group chats are hard to search, memory is unreliable, and generic AI lacks trusted personal context. As a result, people spend too much time deciding, still make disappointing choices, and often forget what was actually good.

## Core insight
The best dining decisions often do not come from the crowd. They come from a small number of real-life people whose taste you trust in specific contexts. One friend may be great for casual Indian, another for date night, another for kid-friendly spots. That trust is highly valuable, but today it lives in scattered memory, text threads, and one-off conversations.

## Why now
In an AI world, generic search, review summarization, and broad recommendations become easier and cheaper. What remains differentiated is trusted human taste, real-world context, and remembered experience. This product combines those into a decision tool for dining.

## Who it is for
People who:
- regularly eat out
- rely on a few real-life friends for restaurant advice
- want fewer disappointing meals
- care more about trusted recommendations than public reviews
- want to remember what was actually good and what to order

## What the app is
- a private trusted taste network
- a decision-support tool for meals
- a memory layer for places you’ve been and what was good
- a way to turn trusted recommendations into something usable at decision time

## What the app is not
- a public review site
- a follower/social graph
- a place to broadcast where you went
- a generic restaurant directory
- a broad social food app
- a long-form restaurant journaling product

## Product principles

### 1. Small network, high trust
The network should stay small, intentional, and based on people you know in real life. The product should optimize for trust and relevance, not network size.

### 2. Decisions over posting
The app should help users decide:
- where to go
- what to order
- what fits the moment

Sharing and logging exist to improve future decisions, not to create a social feed.

### 3. Capture what matters
The most valuable signals are:
- did you like it?
- would you go back?
- what was the best dish?
- what is it good for?

Do not optimize for long reviews or unnecessary structure.

### 4. Capture first, enrich later
Save the visit quickly. Ask optional follow-ups after the visit is already captured.

### 5. Trust should feel human
The app should help users understand:
- who they trust
- what they trust them for
- why their recommendations matter

It should feel intimate and human, not abstract or algorithmic.

## Screen roles

### Home = Decide
Home should help users make a better meal decision quickly.

It should answer:
- what’s worth eating tonight?
- what do my trusted people recommend?
- what should I order?
- what fits this moment?

It should not feel like:
- an empty feed
- a generic dashboard
- a passive activity stream

### Search = Find
Search should help users look up a restaurant and get trusted signal.

It should prioritize:
- restaurants from your table
- your past places
- trusted recommendations
- then broader restaurant search

It should always feel restaurant-specific and curated.

### My Places = Remember
My Places should be a personal memory layer.

It should answer:
- where have I been?
- when did I go?
- what stood out?
- what would I go back to?

It should be visit-based, chronological, and personal.

### Table = Trust
Table should help users know whose taste matters for what.

It should answer:
- who do I trust for date night?
- who is good for family meals?
- who usually knows the right casual spot?

It should feel curated, intimate, and real-life.

### Profile = Taste identity
Profile should summarize the user’s taste.

It should answer:
- what kind of places do I like?
- what dishes define my taste?
- what do I keep going back to?
- what do I contribute to the table?

It should not feel like an account/settings page.

## Core flow

### Main loop
Invite → decide → log → strengthen the trust graph

### Logging flow
Required:
1. select restaurant
2. how was it?
3. save

Optional after save:
- best dish
- would you go back?
- worth recommending?
- what is it good for?
- quick note

### Why logging exists
Logging is not the goal.
Logging improves:
- future decisions
- memory
- trusted recommendations
- confidence in what to order

## Long-term ambition
Over time, the app should become the trusted decision layer for dining within a small real-life network: not just where to go, but what to order, when to trust whom, and what is reliably worth repeating.

## Design direction
- premium
- calm
- iOS-native
- dark-mode friendly
- warm, not performative
- curated, not generic
- small-circle energy, not social-network energy

## Success test
A good test for the app is:

**If I open this app at meal time with friends, does it help me avoid a bad choice and know what to order?**

If the answer is no, the product is drifting.
